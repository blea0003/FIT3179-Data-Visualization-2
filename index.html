Australian Population Analysis (2001-2020)

An basic and general overview of Australia's Population.

Geographical Distribution of Australian Population

Australia is a sparsely populated country relative to it's massive size.

Interstate Migration

It's no surprise that New South Wales, Queensland and Victoria have the most internal migration activity every year. Given that they are where the overwhelming majority of Australians reside.

Though there is a net outflow from these South-Eastern States to neighbouring states. Meaning that in the future, the South and North-East of Australia would be more densely populated.

Playing with the 'Year' slider shows an interesting trend regarding the internal migration intensity.

We find that the intensity patterns are more or less consistent. Meaning the popularity of states has mostly stayed the same over the past two decades.

Population Growth

For the past 2 decades, Australia's population has experienced slow and stagnated growth.

Excepting a slight spike in activity during 2006-2009, growth has been constant.

The majority of states grow by by < 3% annually.

Western Australia and Tasmania's growth differ from the general trend. But that is mostly due to the fact that they are sparesly populated.

Birth vs Death Rate

Here we see the reason for the stagnated population growth.

The average death rate has been almost constant for the past two decades.

However, the already relatively low birth rate has been steadily decreasing since the initial slight increase during the late 2000's.

By 2020, the average birth rate has fallen back below the initial value from year 2000

Gender & Age Distribution

Australia has a very even distribution of females to males for all ages (below 75) and has plenty of room to grow.

In theory, this makes a higher population growth rate possible. However, as we've seen earlier, this is not the case.

Having a low population growth due to low birth rates, coupled with a stagnant death rate, Australia is seeing the trend of an ageing population.

The blue lines in the visualization (right) represent the 1st and 3rd quartile respectively.

From the year 2000 to 2020, both quartiles have increased by an age bracket of 5 years. A significant amount.

If this trend continues, Australia will steadily progress towards a population where the elderly outnumber the young.
